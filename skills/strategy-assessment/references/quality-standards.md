# Quality Standards for Strategy Work

This reference defines what "good" looks like for each section of a strategy document. Use it to calibrate feedback and recognize when work is landing.

---

## Overall Strategy Quality

### Strong strategy feels like:
- **A point of view** — it takes a stance, doesn't hedge
- **Stirring** — it gets you nodding along, creates momentum
- **Tight** — every word earns its place
- **Defensible** — it's rigorous without trying too hard to prove it's smart
- **Grounded** — backed by human insight, not abstractions
- **Clear** — complex thinking, simple expression
- **Actionable** — you know what to do after reading it

### Weak strategy feels like:
- Safe and predictable
- Dense and hard to follow
- Technically correct but uninspiring
- All theory, no action
- Generic enough to apply to anyone

---

## The Business Case / Context

### What it should do:
Establish stakes and urgency. Answer "why this, why now, why care?"

### Strong looks like:
- Clear commercial problem or opportunity
- Specific about what's at stake (not vague "growth")
- Builds tension through contradiction (market growing, brand shrinking)
- Uses data to create urgency, not just inform
- Client should feel pressure to act

### Weak looks like:
- Category overview without implications
- Data dump without "so what"
- No clear business problem
- Academic rather than urgent

### Questions to ask:
- Why should the client spend money on this?
- What happens if they do nothing?
- Is the commercial logic clear?

---

## The Audience

### What it should do:
Paint a vivid, specific picture of who we're talking to and how they actually relate to the category.

### Strong looks like:
- Behavioral specificity over demographics
- Clear relationship to the category (how they use it, what they believe about it)
- Contradiction visible (what they say vs. what they do)
- Feels like a real person, not a persona
- Specific enough that competitors couldn't claim the same audience

### Weak looks like:
- Demographics as primary descriptor
- Generic lifestyle descriptions ("busy professionals")
- Could apply to any brand in the category
- No sense of how they actually behave

### Questions to ask:
- Can I picture this person?
- How do they actually live with this category?
- What's the tension in their behavior?

---

## The Problem

### What it should do:
Frame the specific perceptual or behavioral challenge we're solving. Not a business symptom — a human problem.

### Strong looks like:
- Moves from symptom to cause
- Framed from the consumer's perspective
- Something marketing can actually address
- Specific enough to direct strategy
- Creates an "aha" when paired with the insight

### Weak looks like:
- Business metrics framed as problems ("sales are down")
- Too broad or too narrow
- Problems that require product changes, not marketing
- Generic category problems

### Questions to ask:
- Is this the cause or the symptom?
- Can marketing solve this, or does it need product/pricing/distribution?
- Is this specific to us or generic to the category?

---

## The Insight

### What it should do:
Reveal a truth that makes the strategy obvious. A contradiction or tension that, once seen, changes everything.

### Strong looks like:
- Genuinely surprising (even to category experts)
- Reveals contradiction (say one thing, do another)
- Specific to this audience and category
- Makes the strategy feel inevitable
- Grounded in real behavior, not assumption

### Weak looks like:
- Observation dressed as insight ("people want convenience")
- Restatement of the problem
- Generic enough for any category
- Doesn't surprise or shift perspective

### Questions to ask:
- Is this a discovery or a given?
- Does it make the strategy obvious?
- What's the contradiction?

---

## The Proposition / Strategic Idea

### What it should do:
Articulate the core shift or promise in one clear thought. The single thing the strategy must communicate.

### Strong looks like:
- Flows obviously from the insight
- Single-minded (one idea, not three)
- Ownable by this brand (not generic)
- Directs creative without prescribing execution
- You know what the work should feel like

### Weak looks like:
- Doesn't connect to the insight
- Multiple ideas competing
- Generic benefit language
- Too abstract to direct creative
- Sounds like a tagline rather than a strategy

### Questions to ask:
- Does this flow from the insight?
- Could a competitor own this?
- If I'm a creative team, do I know what to make?

---

## The Organizing Idea / Creative Platform

### What it should do:
Show how the proposition comes to life. The strategic expression that guides all executions.

### Strong looks like:
- Executes the proposition (not just decorates it)
- Works across formats and channels
- Opens creative possibilities
- Rooted in brand truth
- You can immediately imagine work from it

### Weak looks like:
- Assumes a specific format ("a TikTok series about...")
- Restates the proposition without executing it
- Disconnected from how the brand actually operates
- More tactical than strategic

### Questions to ask:
- Does this execute the proposition or just repeat it?
- Can I imagine this across multiple formats?
- Is this idea specific to this brand?

---

## Success Measures

### What it should do:
Define what changes if the strategy works. Not activity metrics — outcome metrics.

### Strong looks like:
- Tests whether the strategic shift happened
- Starts with human outcomes (think/feel/do differently)
- Clear connection to the proposition
- Distinguishes leading (perception) from lagging (business) indicators
- Honest about what's measurable

### Weak looks like:
- Standard KPI list with no strategic connection
- Business metrics without human translation
- You could hit the numbers without the strategy working
- No connection to the proposed shift

### Questions to ask:
- Do these measures prove the strategy worked?
- What changes in human behavior if we succeed?
- Could we hit these numbers and still fail?

---

## Narrative Flow

### What it should do:
Build a persuasive argument that pulls the reader through. Each section should set up the next.

### Strong looks like:
- Clear momentum from slide to slide
- Each section earns its place
- Reader feels pulled through, not pushed
- Transitions feel inevitable
- Could present without the deck

### Weak looks like:
- Slides feel like standalone points
- Abrupt transitions
- No sense of build
- Reader has to connect the dots themselves
- Deck reads like a document, not a presentation

### Questions to ask:
- Is there a clear narrative arc?
- Does each section set up the next?
- Would this be compelling to present?

---

## Using This Reference

When assessing strategy work:

1. **First pass:** Check overall feel. Is this stirring, clear, actionable?
2. **Section review:** Check each section against these standards
3. **Logic check:** Does the chain hold? Context → Problem → Insight → Proposition → Idea → Measures
4. **Narrative check:** Does it flow? Does it build?

Name what's working as clearly as what isn't. Help them protect their strengths while fixing weaknesses.
