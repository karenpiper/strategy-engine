# Questioning Guide

This reference provides the specific questions and pushes to use when assessing strategy work. Organized by section and by intensity level.

---

## Intensity Model

Not all feedback needs the same force. Calibrate based on how far off the work is:

**Level 1 — Nudge:** Work is close, needs refinement
> "This is landing, but I wonder if there's a sharper version. What if you..."

**Level 2 — Push:** Work has potential but isn't there yet
> "I see what you're going for, but it's not quite working because... The question to ask yourself is..."

**Level 3 — Challenge:** Work has a real problem that needs addressing
> "This isn't landing yet. Here's what I'm seeing... Before you go further, you need to answer..."

**Level 4 — Stop:** Work is heading in the wrong direction
> "We need to pause here. The fundamental issue is... Let's go back to..."

Most feedback should live at Levels 2-3. Level 1 is for polish; Level 4 is rare but necessary.

## Business Case / Context

**What you're checking:**
- Is there a clear commercial rationale?
- Is there urgency and stakes?
- Does the data create tension or just inform?

**Diagnostic questions:**
- Why should the client spend money on this?
- What happens if they do nothing?
- Why now, not six months from now?
- What's at stake commercially?

**Pushes by level:**

*Level 1 — Nudge:*
> "The business case is clear. Could you sharpen the 'why now'? What makes this urgent rather than important?"

*Level 2 — Push:*
> "I understand the market context, but I'm not feeling the pressure. What's the tension here? What's the consequence of inaction?"

*Level 3 — Challenge:*
> "I'm not seeing the business case. You've described the category, but why does this matter commercially? What problem are we solving for the client's business?"

*Level 4 — Stop:*
> "Before we go further — what's the commercial objective here? I can't assess the strategy without knowing what business problem it's meant to solve."

## Audience

**What you're checking:**
- Is this behaviorally specific or demographically generic?
- Is there a clear relationship to the category?
- Is there contradiction or tension visible?

**Diagnostic questions:**
- Can I picture this person?
- How do they actually live with this category?
- What's the gap between what they say and what they do?
- Could a competitor claim this same audience?

**Pushes by level:**

*Level 1 — Nudge:*
> "The audience is clear. Could you add more behavioral texture? How do they actually engage with the category day-to-day?"

*Level 2 — Push:*
> "This feels a bit demographic. I know who they are, but I don't know how they think or behave. What's their actual relationship to this category?"

*Level 3 — Challenge:*
> "This audience description could apply to any brand in the category. What specifically about how this person thinks or behaves makes them *your* audience? Where's the tension in their behavior?"

*Level 4 — Stop:*
> "I can't assess the strategy without a clearer audience. Right now this is a demographic, not a person. Go back and ask: How does this person actually live with this category? What do they believe? What's the contradiction?"

## Problem

**What you're checking:**
- Is this a cause or a symptom?
- Is it framed from the consumer's perspective?
- Can marketing actually address this?

**Diagnostic questions:**
- Is this the real problem or a symptom of it?
- Why is this happening? (Keep asking until you hit the human reason)
- Can marketing solve this, or does it need product/pricing/distribution?
- Is this specific to us or generic to the category?

**Pushes by level:**

*Level 1 — Nudge:*
> "The problem is clear. Is there a layer underneath? What's causing this behavior or perception?"

*Level 2 — Push:*
> "This reads like a business symptom — sales down, awareness low. But what's the human problem underneath? What changed in how people think about or use this category?"

*Level 3 — Challenge:*
> "You've identified what's happening, but not why. 'Sales are down' is a symptom. The problem we can solve is perceptual or behavioral. What's the consumer's lived experience of this problem?"

*Level 4 — Stop:*
> "This isn't a marketing problem — it's a [product/pricing/distribution] problem. Marketing can't solve this. Either reframe to what marketing can address, or flag this as a constraint in the brief."

## Insight

**What you're checking:**
- Is this a discovery or a given?
- Is there genuine contradiction or tension?
- Does it make the strategy feel inevitable?

**Diagnostic questions:**
- Is this surprising? Would a category expert say "I hadn't thought of it that way"?
- What's the contradiction? (Say vs. do, want vs. need, believe vs. behave)
- Does this make the strategy obvious?
- Could this insight work for a competitor, or is it specific to us?

**Pushes by level:**

*Level 1 — Nudge:*
> "There's something here. Can you make the tension more explicit? What's the contradiction you've found?"

*Level 2 — Push:*
> "This feels more like an observation than an insight. 'People want X' is true, but it's not surprising. What's the tension underneath? What do they say vs. what do they actually do?"

*Level 3 — Challenge:*
> "This isn't an insight yet — it's a restatement of the problem in different words. An insight should reveal something true that wasn't obvious before. What's the contradiction you've discovered about this audience?"

*Level 4 — Stop:*
> "The insight is the engine of the strategy, and right now it's not running. Before we assess anything else: What's the surprising truth about this audience? What's the gap between what they say and what they do?"

## Proposition / Strategic Idea

**What you're checking:**
- Does it flow from the insight?
- Is it single-minded?
- Is it ownable by this brand?
- Does it direct creative without prescribing it?

**Diagnostic questions:**
- If I accept the insight, does this proposition follow logically?
- Could a competitor own this same proposition?
- If I'm a creative team, do I know what to make?
- Is this one idea or several competing?

**Pushes by level:**

*Level 1 — Nudge:*
> "The proposition is strong. Is there a tighter way to say it? Every word should be earning its place."

*Level 2 — Push:*
> "I see the proposition, but I don't see how it flows from the insight. Walk me through the connection — what shift does the insight make possible?"

*Level 3 — Challenge:*
> "This proposition feels generic — a competitor could own it. What makes this specifically true for this brand? And I'm not sure it flows from your insight. If I accept [insight], why does [proposition] follow?"

*Level 4 — Stop:*
> "The proposition isn't connected to the insight. It feels like you wrote them separately. Go back: Given this insight about the audience, what shift in their thinking, feeling, or behavior would solve the problem? That's your proposition."

## Organizing Idea / Creative Platform

**What you're checking:**
- Does it execute the proposition or just decorate it?
- Can it work across formats?
- Is it an idea or a tactic?

**Diagnostic questions:**
- Does this bring the proposition to life, or just restate it?
- Can I imagine this working across different media?
- Is this a strategic idea or a specific execution?
- Why is this idea true for this brand?

**Pushes by level:**

*Level 1 — Nudge:*
> "The idea is working. Is there a way to make it feel more ownable by this brand? What makes it specifically theirs?"

*Level 2 — Push:*
> "This feels more like a tactic than an organizing idea. 'A TikTok series about X' is an execution. What's the strategic idea that could work across any format?"

*Level 3 — Challenge:*
> "I don't see how this idea executes the proposition. The proposition is [X], the idea is [Y] — they feel disconnected. What about this idea specifically embodies or proves the proposition?"

*Level 4 — Stop:*
> "This isn't an organizing idea — it's a media format or a single execution. An organizing idea is how the brand shows up differently to prove the proposition. Go back and ask: If this proposition is true, how should the brand behave?"

## Success Measures

**What you're checking:**
- Do they test whether the strategic shift happened?
- Are they connected to the proposition?
- Could you hit them without the strategy working?

**Diagnostic questions:**
- What changes in human behavior if this works?
- Do these measures prove the proposition worked, or just that we did stuff?
- Could you achieve these numbers and still fail the strategy?

**Pushes by level:**

*Level 1 — Nudge:*
> "The measures are solid. Could you add a leading indicator that tests perception shift, not just behavior?"

*Level 2 — Push:*
> "These are activity metrics — reach, impressions, engagement. But what changes in how the audience thinks or feels? How would you know if the strategic shift actually happened?"

*Level 3 — Challenge:*
> "These measures don't test your strategy. You could hit every one of these numbers and still not know if the proposition landed. What would be different about the audience if this strategy works?"

*Level 4 — Stop:*
> "The success measures are disconnected from the strategy. Before you finalize: If this strategy works, what will be true about the audience that isn't true today? Design measures that test that shift."

## Narrative Flow

**What you're checking:**
- Does it build momentum?
- Are transitions clear?
- Does each section earn its place?

**Diagnostic questions:**
- Is there a clear arc from beginning to end?
- Does each section set up the next?
- Could you present this without the deck and still be compelling?
- Where do I lose the thread?

**Pushes by level:**

*Level 1 — Nudge:*
> "The story is there. A few transitions feel abrupt — especially between [X] and [Y]. Can you make the connective logic more explicit?"

*Level 2 — Push:*
> "I'm losing the thread in places. This feels more like a collection of slides than a building argument. What's the narrative arc? How does each section set up the next?"

*Level 3 — Challenge:*
> "The individual sections have good thinking, but they're not adding up to a story. I can't see how [section A] leads to [section B]. Before you polish the parts, fix the whole."

*Level 4 — Stop:*
> "There's no narrative here — it's a series of points, not an argument. Step back from the slides. What's the story you're trying to tell? Write that out in prose first, then rebuild the deck."

## Unlocking Stuck Thinking

When someone is stuck and keeps circling, try these reframes:

**For insight:**
> "Forget the framework for a second. What's the most interesting thing you learned about this audience? What surprised you?"

**For proposition:**
> "If you had to text a creative director the strategy in one sentence, what would you say?"

**For simplicity:**
> "Pretend you have 30 seconds with the client in an elevator. What's the strategy?"

**For business case:**
> "If the CFO asked 'why should we spend money on this?' — what's your answer?"

**For narrative:**
> "Close the deck. Tell me the story out loud. Don't describe slides — just tell me what you want me to believe by the end."

**For bravery:**
> "What's the version of this you'd be proud of but are afraid to present? Let's look at that."

---

## Using This Guide

Start with diagnostic questions to understand where the work is.

Match your intensity to the gap — don't use Level 4 force on a Level 2 problem.

Always give a path forward. The push should include a question they can answer or a move they can try.

If they defend their choice, engage genuinely. They may have context you don't. But if the logic still doesn't hold, name that clearly.
