# Weak Thinking Patterns

This reference catalogs common patterns that weaken strategy work. For each pattern: what it looks like, why it's a problem, and how to coach through it.

---

## Pattern 1: Missing Business Case

**What it looks like:**
- Strategy jumps straight to creative territory without establishing commercial stakes
- No clear answer to "why should the client spend money on this?"
- Lots of brand thinking, no business thinking
- The "so what?" is missing

**Why it's weak:**
Strategy exists to solve business problems. Without a clear commercial rationale, the work feels like an academic exercise. Clients buy solutions to problems, not interesting thinking.

**How to coach:**
> "I'm not seeing the business case here. What's the commercial problem this solves? Why does this matter to the client's bottom line?"

> "The strategic thinking is interesting, but walk me back to the business objective. What changes commercially if this works?"

---

## Pattern 2: Too Many Words / Lack of Simplicity

**What it looks like:**
- Dense slides with paragraphs of text
- Multiple ideas competing on the same page
- Hedging language ("somewhat," "in a way," "to some extent")
- Repetition of the same point in different words
- Strategy that takes 20 slides to say what could be said in 5

**Why it's weak:**
If you can't say it simply, you haven't finished thinking. Complexity often masks unclear thinking. Clients and creative teams lose the thread.

**How to coach:**
> "There's a lot here. What's the one thing you need me to take away from this section?"

> "I'm reading a lot of words but I'm not sure what the core idea is. Can you give me the single sentence version?"

> "This feels like you're still working out what you think. What would this look like if you were 100% confident in your direction?"

---

## Pattern 3: Technically Correct but Creatively Dead

**What it looks like:**
- Strategy that follows all the rules but doesn't excite
- Logical but predictable
- No point of view, no provocation
- Could have been written by any agency for any client
- Safe choices throughout

**Why it's weak:**
Strategy should open creative possibilities, not close them. If the strategy doesn't stir anyone, the creative work won't either. Correctness isn't enough.

**How to coach:**
> "This is logically sound, but it's not making me lean forward. Where's the provocation? What's the point of view that only this brand could own?"

> "I could see three other agencies presenting this same strategy. What makes this distinctly yours? What's the brave choice?"

> "What would a bolder version of this look like? I'm not saying go there, but I want to know you considered it."

---

## Pattern 4: Recursive Strategy (Restating the Brief)

**What it looks like:**
- Strategy that essentially repeats what the client already said
- "The problem is [brief's problem]. The solution is [brief's objective]."
- No new thinking or reframing
- Insight that's just the brief in different words

**Why it's weak:**
The client hired you to add value, not to echo them. Strategy should reframe the problem or reveal something the client didn't see. If you're just restating, you haven't done the work.

**How to coach:**
> "This reads like the brief back to the client. What's your added perspective? What do you see that they didn't?"

> "Where did your thinking depart from what the client gave you? That's usually where the value is."

---

## Pattern 5: Over-Philosophizing Without Action

**What it looks like:**
- Lots of theory, frameworks, and "laddering up"
- Beautiful thinking that never lands in "so this is what we'll do"
- Decks that end with principles rather than recommendations
- Abstract language that sounds smart but doesn't direct action

**Why it's weak:**
Strategy is a bridge between insight and action. If it stays in the clouds, creative teams don't know what to make and clients don't know what they're buying.

**How to coach:**
> "There's interesting thinking here, but I'm not seeing where it lands. What does this actually mean we do? What's the recommendation?"

> "If I'm a creative team reading this, what do I make? The philosophy is clear but the direction isn't."

> "Less 'what we believe' and more 'what we'll do.' Can you make that concrete?"

---

## Pattern 6: Over-Engineering

**What it looks like:**
- Unnecessary complexity in frameworks and models
- Multiple overlapping strategic constructs
- Proprietary terminology that doesn't add meaning
- Strategy that's impressive but impractical
- More scaffolding than building

**Why it's weak:**
Over-engineering often signals insecurity — trying to look smart rather than be useful. Complex frameworks rarely survive contact with creative development.

**How to coach:**
> "There's a lot of scaffolding here. Which of these constructs is actually doing work, and which could we remove without losing anything?"

> "I'm getting lost in the framework. Can you strip this back to the essential strategic move?"

> "What would this look like if you trusted the thinking more and the framework less?"

---

## Pattern 7: Symptom vs. Cause Confusion

**What it looks like:**
- Problem framed as business metrics: "Sales are down," "Awareness is low"
- No explanation of the human reason behind the symptom
- Jumping to solutions without understanding root causes

**Why it's weak:**
Business symptoms don't tell you what to do. Understanding *why* sales are down — the perceptual or behavioral shift — is what enables strategy.

**How to coach:**
> "Sales being down is the symptom. What's the cause? What changed in how people think about or use this category?"

> "You've identified what's happening. Now tell me why it's happening."

---

## Pattern 8: Generic Audience Description

**What it looks like:**
- Demographics as primary descriptor: "Millennials," "Busy professionals"
- Descriptions that could apply to any brand in the category
- No behavioral specificity or contradiction
- Psychographics that are just demographics in disguise

**Why it's weak:**
Generic audiences produce generic strategies. If you don't understand how this specific person relates to this specific category, you can't find a meaningful insight.

**How to coach:**
> "This could describe the audience for your competitor. What specifically about how this person thinks or behaves makes them your audience?"

> "Demographics tell me who they are. Tell me how they actually live with this category. What's the contradiction in their behavior?"

---

## Pattern 9: Surface Insight

**What it looks like:**
- "Insights" that are actually observations: "People want convenience"
- Restatements of the problem dressed as insight
- Nothing surprising or perspective-shifting
- Insights that could apply to dozens of categories

**Why it's weak:**
An insight should make the strategy obvious. If it doesn't surprise you or change how you think about the problem, it's not doing its job.

**How to coach:**
> "That's an observation, but is it an insight? What's the contradiction you've found? What's true that isn't being said?"

> "I'm not surprised by this. What makes it a discovery rather than a given?"

> "An insight should make the strategy feel inevitable. Does this do that?"

---

## Pattern 10: Logical Disconnects

**What it looks like:**
- Insight that doesn't lead to the proposition
- Proposition that doesn't lead to the idea
- Strategy sections that could be shuffled without anyone noticing
- "Leap of faith" connections between sections

**Why it's weak:**
Strategy is a chain of logic. If the links don't hold, the whole thing falls apart. Creative teams will feel the disconnect even if they can't name it.

**How to coach:**
> "I see the insight. I see the proposition. But I don't see how one leads to the other. Walk me through the connection."

> "If I accept this insight as true, why does this proposition follow? Make the logic explicit."

---

## Pattern 11: Context Without Stakes

**What it looks like:**
- Category information presented as backdrop
- No clear "why now" or sense of urgency
- Data without implications
- Context that reads like a Wikipedia entry

**Why it's weak:**
Context should build tension and establish stakes. If it doesn't create urgency, it's just filler.

**How to coach:**
> "I understand the market now, but I don't feel the pressure. Why does this require action? What's at stake?"

> "What's the implication of these facts? Don't just describe — create tension."

---

## Pattern 12: Disconnected Success Measures

**What it looks like:**
- KPI lists with no connection to strategy
- Business metrics that don't test the strategic shift
- Measures you could achieve without the strategy working
- Generic: reach, impressions, engagement

**Why it's weak:**
If your measures don't test whether the strategic shift happened, you'll never know if the strategy worked.

**How to coach:**
> "These measures tell me if we reached people. They don't tell me if we shifted them. What would change in how the audience thinks or behaves if this strategy works?"

> "Could you hit these numbers and still fail the strategy? If yes, they're the wrong measures."

---

## Pattern 13: Narrative That Doesn't Flow

**What it looks like:**
- Slides that feel like standalone points rather than a building argument
- Abrupt transitions between sections
- No sense of momentum or build
- Reader has to work to connect the dots

**Why it's weak:**
Strategy is persuasion. If the story doesn't flow, you lose people. They should feel pulled through the deck, not pushed.

**How to coach:**
> "I'm losing the thread between [section A] and [section B]. What's the connective logic?"

> "This feels like a collection of slides, not a story. What's the narrative arc? How does each section set up the next?"

---

## Using This Reference

When reviewing strategy work, scan for these patterns. They often cluster — a missing business case often comes with over-philosophizing; a generic audience often produces a surface insight.

Name the pattern when you see it, but always give a path forward. The goal is to help them see the weakness and know how to fix it.
