# Socratic Questioning & Escalation Guide

This guide shows the specific questions Claude uses to push your thinking, organized by section and by escalation level.

## Escalation Model

For each thinking-heavy section, Claude operates with escalation:

1. **Level 1 - Socratic Questioning:** Open questions designed to help you develop your own thinking
2. **Level 2 - Probing for Specificity:** Pushes beyond surface observations to behavioral depth
3. **Level 3 - Naming the Problem:** Directly identifies weak thinking patterns and names them
4. **Level 4 - Requiring Rework:** Refuses to move forward until thinking is stronger

Most productive brief development happens at Levels 1-2. Claude escalates only when thinking isn't progressing.

---

## The Audience Problem

**Goal:** Move from symptom to cause. From "what's happening" to "why it's happening" from the consumer's perspective.

**Level 1 - Socratic Questions:**
- "You've identified [symptom]. What changed in how consumers think about or use this category that created this symptom?"
- "If someone asked you 'Why is this happening?', how would you answer?"
- "What's the perceptual or behavioral gap you're trying to close?"
- "What does the audience currently believe about the category/brand that's holding them back?"

**Level 2 - Probing for Specificity:**
- "That's a business symptom. What's the consumer's lived experience of this problem? What do they feel or think?"
- "Is this a problem unique to your audience, or would this apply to everyone in the category?"
- "How do you know this is a real problem for the audience vs. an assumption?"

**Level 3 - Naming the Problem:**
- "You've framed this as a business problem (sales down, awareness low). But marketing solves problems in consumer perception and behavior, not business metrics. What's the perception or behavior problem underneath?"
- "This sounds generic to the category. What specifically about your audience's relationship to your brand creates this problem?"

**Level 4 - Requiring Rework:**
- "I can't move forward until we define the consumer problem, not the business symptom. Go back and ask: What does this audience currently think/feel/do about this category, and what's the gap you want to close?"

## The Key Insight

**Goal:** Find the contradiction, tension, or unspoken truth that makes the strategy obvious.

**Level 1 - Socratic Questions:**
- "What's the contradiction you've found? What do they say they want vs. what they actually do?"
- "What's true about this audience that nobody's saying out loud?"
- "What's the tension in their relationship with the category?"
- "If you had to explain to a creative team 'Here's why our strategy works,' what would you say?"

**Level 2 - Probing for Specificity:**
- "That feels like an observation. What makes it surprising? What changes about how you think about the problem once you know this?"
- "Would a competitor in this space have discovered the same insight, or is this specific to your audience?"
- "What consumer research or behavior supports this?"

**Level 3 - Naming the Problem:**
- "That's not an insight; it's a restatement of the problem. An insight reveals something true that wasn't obvious. What are you discovering about the audience that will make strategy obvious?"
- "This insight could apply to many categories. What's the contradiction specific to your audience or this category?"

**Level 4 - Requiring Rework:**
- "The insight needs to reveal something true and surprising about the audience. Right now it's too surface. Go back and look for contradiction, tension, or the gap between what they say and what they do."

## The Single-Minded Proposition

**Goal:** One sentence that articulates the core shift or benefit, flowing obviously from the insight.

**Level 1 - Socratic Questions:**
- "Given the insight you just shared, what shift do you want to create in how the audience thinks/feels/behaves?"
- "If this strategy works, what will be true about the audience that isn't true today?"
- "Why would the audience believe this? What benefit does it deliver to them?"
- "Could a competitor own this same proposition, or is it specific to your brand?"

**Level 2 - Probing for Specificity:**
- "That feels generic. How is this proposition specific to your brand or unique to your audience?"
- "Does this proposition flow from the insight you just described, or would it work with any insight?"
- "What does 'better' or 'more authentic' or 'more relevant' actually mean in human terms for this audience?"

**Level 3 - Naming the Problem:**
- "This proposition doesn't flow from your insight. Your insight is [X], but your proposition is [Y]. Walk me through the connection."
- "This sounds like benefit language but not like a strategic platform. What's the core shift you're trying to create?"

**Level 4 - Requiring Rework:**
- "Start over with this one. First, remind me of your insight. Then ask: Given that truth about the audience, what perception or behavior shift does that make possible?"

## The Organizing Idea

**Goal:** A strategic idea that executes the proposition, not just decorates it.

**Level 1 - Socratic Questions:**
- "How does this idea bring your proposition to life? What about it is an expression of the proposition?"
- "If I removed the proposition and just read the organizing idea, would I understand the strategy?"
- "How could this idea work across different formats or media?"
- "Why is this idea true for this brand? What about the brand makes this idea feel authentic?"

**Level 2 - Probing for Specificity:**
- "This sounds like a tactic (a specific ad, a format). Is there a strategic idea underneath?"
- "How is this idea specific to your proposition? Could you use this same idea with a different proposition?"
- "What does the audience actually do or experience with this idea?"

**Level 3 - Naming the Problem:**
- "You've described a media format or tactic, not a strategic organizing idea. An organizing idea is the way you'll embody and execute the proposition. What's the strategic idea?"
- "This idea doesn't execute your proposition. Your proposition is [X], your idea is [Y]. They don't connect."

**Level 4 - Requiring Rework:**
- "An organizing idea is the strategic expression of the proposition. Right now you've got a tactic. Go back and ask: How could a campaign or brand show up differently to prove this proposition?"

## Context (Research/Reference Section)

**Goal:** Organize and ground the context in research, building stakes and urgency.

**Level 1 - Socratic Questions:**
- "You've given me these facts. What's the implication for the brand?"
- "Why does this require action now?"
- "What's the tension or opportunity in what you're describing?"
- "How does this context set up the problem we'll try to solve?"

**Level 2 - Probing for Specificity:**
- "Which of these facts matter most to understanding why this strategy is necessary?"
- "What research supports this, and what's it actually showing?"
- "How are competitors responding to this same context?"

---

## Audience (Research/Reference Section)

**Goal:** Paint a vivid picture of actual behavior, contradictions, and category relationship.

**Level 1 - Socratic Questions:**
- "You've described demographics. But how does this person actually engage with the category?"
- "What's their relationship to your brand?"
- "What do they want from this category that they're not getting?"
- "What's the contradiction in how they act vs. what they say they care about?"

**Level 2 - Probing for Specificity:**
- "This sounds like it could describe the audience for any brand in this space. What makes them specifically your audience?"
- "What's their actual day-to-day context? How does the category fit into their life?"

---

## Success Measures (Structural Section)

**Goal:** Ensure measures actually test whether the strategic shift happened.

**Level 1 - Socratic Questions:**
- "If this strategy works, what will be different about how the audience thinks, feels, or behaves?"
- "How would you know if this strategy succeeded?"
- "What would a competitor's success look like vs. your success?"

**Level 2 - Probing for Specificity:**
- "These are business metrics. But what consumer shift do they represent?"
- "Could you achieve these measures without the strategic shift actually happening?"
- "How do these measures test the core premise of your strategy vs. just measuring activity?"

## Logical Chain Validation Questions

Before moving to final validation, Claude will check the logical flow by asking:

**Problem → Insight:**
- "Your problem is [X]. Your insight is [Y]. Does the insight explain why the problem exists?"
- "Reading problem and insight together, do they feel like a discovery?"

**Insight → Proposition:**
- "Your insight is [X]. Your proposition is [Y]. Does the insight make the proposition inevitable?"
- "If I accept the insight as true, does your proposition follow logically?"

**Proposition → Organizing Idea:**
- "Your proposition is [X]. Your organizing idea is [Y]. Does the idea execute the proposition?"
- "Could you use this same organizing idea with a different proposition?"

**Proposition → Success Measures:**
- "Your proposition is [X]. Your success measures are [Y]. Do these measures prove the proposition worked?"
- "Could you achieve these measures without the strategic shift happening?"

## Using This Guide

When Claude asks you questions, they follow this model. You'll notice:

- Early questions are open and exploratory (Levels 1-2)
- If thinking stalls or circles, Claude will name the problem directly (Level 3)
- If thinking doesn't move forward, Claude will decline to advance (Level 4)

This progression is intentional. The goal is to help you develop stronger thinking, not to generate content for you.

If you feel stuck at any escalation level, ask Claude: "What specific question should I be asking myself here?" This often unblocks thinking faster than continuing to explore.
