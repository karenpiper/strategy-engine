# Brief Structure & Quality Standards

This reference describes each section of the strategic brief, what makes it breakthrough-ready vs. weak, and how to ground it in 4Cs reality (Consumer, Category, Company, Competitors).

## The Assignment

**What it is:** The high-level creative deliverable and scope. What does the creative team need to produce to ensure a successful client presentation?

**Breakthrough thinking:**
- Clearly defined scope (e.g., "full brand platform launch" vs. vague "marketing campaign")
- Acknowledges constraints (timing, media, product availability)
- Specific enough that a creative team knows what not to do

**Weak thinking:**
- Overly broad ("do something amazing")
- Disconnected from the business opportunity
- Unclear boundaries

**Grounding:** This comes from the business need and timeline, not aspirational thinking.

---

## The Context

**What it is:** The business background, opportunity, or problem. Why does this assignment exist now? What market shift, competitive pressure, business challenge, or growth opportunity is driving this work?

**Breakthrough thinking:**
- Rooted in 4Cs data (consumer behavior changes, category dynamics, company performance, competitive moves)
- Explains the "why now" with specificity
- Often reveals a tension or contradiction (e.g., "category is growing but our brand is losing share")
- Not just listing facts, but using them to establish stakes

**Weak thinking:**
- Generic statements ("the market is competitive")
- Listing research without implications
- No clear business urgency
- Treating context as backdrop rather than the foundation for strategy

**Grounding:** Pull from research. What in consumer behavior, category trends, competitive landscape, or company performance justifies this brief existing?

---

## Who Are We Talking To?

**What it is:** The target audience painted with behavioral specificity. Not just demographics, but how they actually live, what they value, and how they relate to the category/brand/product.

**Breakthrough thinking:**
- Behavioral specificity over demographic labels ("people who prioritize convenience over cost" vs. "millennials")
- Psychographic detail (values, mindset, lifestyle context)
- Clear relationship to the category (how they currently use it, what they believe about it, gaps they feel)
- Tension or contradiction visible in how they behave vs. what they say they want
- Grounded in consumer observation, not stereotype

**Weak thinking:**
- Age/income/gender as primary descriptors
- Generic lifestyle descriptions
- No sense of how they actually engage with the category
- Audience that could apply to any brand in the category

**Grounding:** This should come from 4Cs consumer research—actual behavior, attitudes, usage patterns.

---

## The Audience Problem

**What it is:** The specific challenge the audience faces that your brand/product can address. This is not a business problem ("sales are down"), it's a perceptual or behavioral problem from the consumer's lens.

**Breakthrough thinking:**
- Moves from symptom to cause (Why are we losing share? Because [deep problem])
- Rooted in audience tension or contradiction (they want X but experience Y)
- Framed as what marketing can solve (a perception, belief, behavior, or mindset gap)
- Specific enough that a different brand couldn't own this same problem
- Clear connection to the category and consumer relationship

**Weak thinking:**
- Business metrics framed as consumer problems ("sales are down")
- Generic category problems ("people don't think about sustainability")
- Problems that aren't addressable through marketing/brand/product
- Too broad or too narrow

**Grounding:** Comes from understanding consumer behavior and mindset. What actual gap exists between what they need/want and what they perceive about the category?

---

## The Key Insight

**What it is:** The unspoken truth that sheds new light on the problem. A contradiction or tension that, once revealed, makes the strategy obvious.

**Breakthrough thinking:**
- Reveals a genuine contradiction (I want X, but I actually do Y)
- Moves beyond the surface observation to the truth underneath
- Often found in the tension between what consumers say and what they do
- Surprising enough to change how you think about the audience
- Directly connected to the problem (insight explains why the problem exists)
- Inspires strategic possibility (once you see this truth, the path forward becomes clearer)

**Weak thinking:**
- Surface observation ("people like convenience")
- Generic insight that applies to many categories or brands
- Disconnected from the problem you just described
- "Insight" that's really just restating the problem
- Too broad to inspire specific strategy

**Grounding:** Comes from 4Cs reality—consumer behavior, category dynamics, audience mindset. The insight should feel like a discovery, not a predetermined conclusion.

---

## The Single-Minded Proposition

**What it is:** One sentence that articulates the core promise or shift your brand is making. This launches creative thinking and establishes the path for the work.

**Breakthrough thinking:**
- Distinct and ownable by your brand (not any brand in the category)
- Engaging and specific (not generic benefit language)
- Flows directly from your insight (insight → proposition is obvious)
- Unambiguous about the benefit or shift
- Motivating to the consumer (speaks to what they actually care about)
- Suggests direction without prescribing execution

**Weak thinking:**
- Generic benefit that applies to many brands ("we're reliable")
- Disconnected from the insight you just developed
- Too clever or obscure
- Doesn't clearly articulate a shift or benefit
- Sounds like a tagline rather than a strategic statement

**Grounding:** The insight should make this proposition feel inevitable, not arbitrary.

---

## The Organizing Idea

**What it is:** The strategic idea that brings the proposition to life. How will the brand/campaign/experience embody and execute the proposition?

**Breakthrough thinking:**
- Executes the proposition (not just decorates it)
- Could work across multiple media and formats
- Specific about the idea but flexible in execution
- Rooted in how the brand actually operates or how the category works
- Creates a reason to believe (why should consumers accept this proposition?)
- Opens creative possibilities rather than limiting them

**Weak thinking:**
- Assumes a media format (e.g., "a TikTok where...")
- Restates the proposition without executing it
- Disconnected from how the brand or category actually works
- So specific it leaves no room for creative variation
- "Idea" that's really a tactic (a specific ad creative, a promotion, etc.)

**Grounding:** The organizing idea should feel like a natural expression of the brand meeting the consumer insight.

---

## How Will We Measure Success?

**What it is:** Not a KPI list, but a clear articulation of what shift in consumer thinking, feeling, or behavior you expect. What should be different about the audience because of this work?

**Breakthrough thinking:**
- Starts with human outcomes (what they think/feel/do differently)
- Ties back to the original problem (does this measure the shift we promised?)
- Specific about direction and ideally magnitude
- Distinguishes between leading indicators (perception/engagement) and lagging indicators (business results)
- Honest about what's measurable and what requires proxy measures
- Makes clear how you'll know if the strategy worked

**Weak thinking:**
- KPI list without strategic intent (reach, impressions, CTR)
- Measures disconnected from your proposition
- Too broad ("increase brand awareness") without specificity
- No connection to the human shift you're trying to create
- Business metrics presented as consumer outcomes

**Grounding:** Success measures should prove that your proposition created the shift you intended.

---

## How Can We Support This?

**What it is:** The features or benefits of the product/brand that substantiate the proposition. Why should the audience believe the organizing idea?

**Breakthrough thinking:**
- Honestly grounded in actual product/brand attributes
- Translated into consumer benefits (not feature lists)
- Shows why the brand is credible on this proposition
- Acknowledges limitations while building the case
- Connects product truth to consumer desire

**Weak thinking:**
- Feature lists without benefit translation
- Overstated claims disconnected from reality
- Generic support that applies to competitors too
- Ignored where the product falls short
- Support section that doesn't actually support the proposition

**Grounding:** This comes from company reality and product truth.

---

## Deliverables & Considerations

**What it is:** The specific outputs needed, timing, media/format requirements, and strategic constraints.

**Includes:**
- What needs to be created (spot, campaign, platform, etc.)
- Timeline and milestones
- Media formats and distribution
- Technical requirements (product changes, compliance, etc.)
- Existing brand DNA or campaign connections to honor
- Tag line, hero assets, or other specific requirements

**Breakthrough thinking:**
- Clear enough that creative teams know what they're building
- Honest about constraints while explaining why they exist
- Acknowledges how this connects to broader brand strategy
- Leaves room for creative interpretation within boundaries

**Weak thinking:**
- Overly prescriptive (turns strategic brief into creative brief)
- Conflicting requirements that handicap the strategy
- Vague timing or scope
- Disconnected from the strategy sections above

---

## Inspiration & Innovation

**What it is:** Reference material that sparks creative possibility. Relevant work from other categories, platform updates, social trends, or strategic thought starters.

**Breakthrough thinking:**
- Relevant to the challenge (not just "cool work")
- Includes diverse references (different categories, media, perspectives)
- Each reference has a strategic reason attached ("This shows how a legacy brand can feel culturally relevant")
- Opens possibility rather than limiting to precedent
- Includes links or examples

**Weak thinking:**
- Random "inspiration" with no connection to the brief
- All reference from direct competitors
- Inspirational without being strategic
- Missing context for why each reference matters

---

## Testing Your Sections for Coherence

**Does Context set up the Problem?**
The business/consumer reality described in Context should make the Problem obvious.

**Does the Problem lead to the Insight?**
The Insight should reveal why the Problem exists. Reading both should feel like a discovery.

**Does the Insight make the Proposition inevitable?**
Once you understand the Insight, the Single-Minded Proposition should feel obvious.

**Does the Organizing Idea execute the Proposition?**
The Organizing Idea should be a natural expression of the Proposition in action.

**Do Success Measures test whether the Proposition worked?**
You should be able to look at success measures and directly see whether the strategic shift happened.

If any of these connections feel loose, go back and strengthen the thinking.
