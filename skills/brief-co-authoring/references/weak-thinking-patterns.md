# Weak Thinking Patterns & How to Spot Them

## Pattern 1: Symptom vs. Cause Confusion

**The Problem:**
Confusing what you observe (symptom) with why it's happening (cause). This shows up most in the "Audience Problem" and "Key Insight" sections.

**How to spot it:**
- Problem statement uses business metrics: "Our sales are down," "We're losing market share," "Brand awareness needs to increase"
- Problem doesn't explain the human reason: Why are sales down? What's changed in how the audience thinks about or uses the category?

**What makes it weak:**
Sales being down is a symptom. The cause might be: "Competitors made this category feel irrelevant to how people actually want to spend time" or "Our brand got associated with an old generation while the audience moved on."

**How Claude will push:**
"You've identified the symptom (sales are down). What's the underlying behavioral or perceptual change that's causing this? Why did consumers shift their behavior?"

**How to fix it:**
Move from metrics to mindset. Ask: What changed in the audience's perception, values, or behavior that created this business symptom?

## Pattern 2: Generic Audience Description

**The Problem:**
Describing the audience through demographics and generic lifestyle instead of behavioral specificity and actual relationship to the category.

**How to spot it:**
- Audience sounds like it could apply to any brand in the category
- Descriptions rely on age, income, or life stage: "Busy professionals," "Millennials," "Mothers 25-45"
- No mention of how they actually engage with the category
- Contradictions and tensions are missing

**Examples of generic:**
- "Young professionals who value efficiency"
- "Health-conscious parents"
- "Tech-savvy consumers"

**What makes it weak:**
These descriptions don't help you understand why your brand specifically matters to them or what they actually believe about your category.

**How Claude will push:**
"This sounds like it could describe the audience for many brands in this space. What specifically about how this person thinks, believes, or behaves makes them your audience? What's their actual relationship to the category?"

**How to fix it:**
Add behavioral specificity and contradiction. "People who say they care about health but grab whatever's convenient for lunch. They feel guilty about the gap between their health intentions and their actual choices."

## Pattern 3: Surface Insight

**The Problem:**
Mistaking an observation or restatement of the problem for an actual insight. Insights should reveal something true that changes how you think about the problem.

**How to spot it:**
- The "insight" doesn't surprise you
- It's generic enough to apply to many categories
- It's essentially a restatement of the problem
- Reading it doesn't make the strategy obvious

**Examples of surface insights:**
- "People want convenience"
- "Consumers care about authenticity"
- "People are busy"
- "Audiences want content that entertains them"

**What makes it weak:**
These might be true, but they don't reveal the contradiction or truth that makes strategy obvious. They're already known.

**How Claude will push:**
"That's an observation, but it's not an insight. An insight reveals something true that wasn't obvious before and makes the strategy suddenly clear. What's the contradiction you've found? What's the unspoken truth about this audience?"

**How to fix it:**
Look for tension or contradiction. Find the gap between what the audience says they want and what they actually do. Find what they're not saying. "People say they want convenience, but what they're really avoiding is feeling judged for taking shortcuts."

## Pattern 4: Insight → Proposition Disconnect

**The Problem:**
The Single-Minded Proposition doesn't flow from or feel inevitable from the Key Insight.

**How to spot it:**
- You could swap the proposition with a competitor's without breaking the brief logic
- The proposition doesn't directly address the insight
- Insight talks about audience mindset; proposition talks about product feature
- You had to explain the connection (it shouldn't need explanation)

**What makes it weak:**
If the insight doesn't make the proposition obvious, the creative team won't understand why you chose this direction. The strategy feels arbitrary.

**How Claude will push:**
"I see the insight about [what you found]. But I don't see how that leads directly to [your proposition]. Walk me through the logical connection."

**How to fix it:**
Ask: "Given this insight about the audience, what shift in their thinking, feeling, or behavior would solve the problem?" That shift is your proposition.

## Pattern 5: Proposition → Organizing Idea Disconnect

**The Problem:**
The Organizing Idea doesn't execute the Single-Minded Proposition; it just decorates it or addresses a different strategic idea.

**How to spot it:**
- Organizing idea sounds like a tactic or media format: "A TikTok series about," "An influencer partnership," "A limited edition product"
- It doesn't express or embody the proposition
- You could drop the organizing idea and the proposition would survive unchanged
- The organizing idea is more specific than the proposition (backward flow)

**What makes it weak:**
The organizing idea is supposed to show how you'll bring the proposition to life. If it doesn't, creative teams will generate their own organizing idea (which might contradict yours).

**How Claude will push:**
"Your proposition is [X]. Your organizing idea is [Y]. I don't see how Y executes X. What about that idea specifically embodies or brings to life the proposition?"

**How to fix it:**
Ask: "If I accept this proposition as true, how would the brand/campaign/experience need to show up differently?" That's your organizing idea.

## Pattern 6: Disconnected Success Measures

**The Problem:**
Success measures don't directly test whether the strategic shift actually happened.

**How to spot it:**
- Success measures are a standard KPI list with no connection to the strategy
- Business metrics without translation to human change
- You could achieve the measures and still fail the strategy
- Measures don't differentiate your strategy from a competitor's strategy

**Examples of weak:**
- Strategy: "Make the brand feel more culturally relevant" | Measures: "Increase impressions by 40%, CTR by 15%"
- Strategy: "Build a reason to believe in quality" | Measures: "Reach 50M people"

**What makes it weak:**
You can't tell if your strategy worked. Impressions don't prove cultural relevance. Reach doesn't prove quality perception.

**How Claude will push:**
"Your strategy is [X]. Your success measures are [Y]. How do these measures prove that X actually happened? What would change in how the audience thinks/feels/behaves if your strategy was successful?"

**How to fix it:**
Start with the shift: "If this strategy works, consumers will think/feel/do [what?]". Then design measures that test that shift.

## Pattern 7: Overstated Support Section

**The Problem:**
Claiming the product/brand can deliver on the proposition when it actually can't, or disconnecting the support from the proposition.

**How to spot it:**
- Product claims feel stretched or overstated
- Ignores where the product is weak
- Support section reads like marketing copy instead of strategic truth
- Doesn't actually support the specific proposition

**What makes it weak:**
Creates a "reason not to believe." If the creative work has to hide or exaggerate the truth to support the proposition, the proposition is wrong.

**How Claude will push:**
"This proposition requires [what from the product/brand]. Can the brand honestly deliver on that? What does the product actually do well that supports this idea?"

**How to fix it:**
Be honest about the product. If the product can't support the proposition, change the proposition to something it can support. Or change the product position if possible.

## Pattern 8: Context Without Stakes

**The Problem:**
Providing research and background information without explaining why it matters or how it creates urgency.

**How to spot it:**
- Context section reads like a research dump
- No clear "why now" or sense of urgency
- Facts are presented but not connected to implications
- You understand the market but not why the brand needs to act

**What makes it weak:**
Without stakes, the assignment feels academic instead of urgent. Creative teams won't understand the pressure.

**How Claude will push:**
"I see the market context you've described. But what's the implication for this brand? Why does this require action now instead of six months from now?"

**How to fix it:**
Use context to build tension. "Category is growing [fact], but our brand is losing share [tension]. Competitors are moving upmarket [threat] while we're stuck in our old position [consequence]."

---

## Pattern 9: Audience Problem That Isn't Addressable

**The Problem:**
Framing a problem as marketing-addressable when it's actually a product problem, pricing problem, or distribution problem.

**How to spot it:**
- The problem requires product changes, not marketing/brand
- The problem is structural (e.g., "our price is too high")
- Marketing can't influence perception because the reality doesn't support it
- Creative work can't solve what's actually a business problem

**What makes it weak:**
Creative teams will struggle because no amount of great work addresses the real problem.

**How Claude will push:**
"The problem you've described is [X]. That feels like a [product/pricing/distribution] problem rather than a marketing problem. Is the assignment to solve this, or to work around it?"

**How to fix it:**
Either reframe the problem to what marketing can address, or clarify in Deliverables & Considerations that the brief acknowledges this structural limitation.

---

## Pattern 10: Missing Category Grounding

**The Problem:**
The brief doesn't reference how the category actually works—its dynamics, conventions, consumer expectations, or competitive landscape.

**How to spot it:**
- Strategy could apply to any category or brand
- No mention of what makes this category unique
- No sense of how competitors operate
- Audience relationship to category is vague

**What makes it weak:**
Without category understanding, you miss leverage points and creative conventions that matter to consumers in this space.

**How Claude will push:**
"How does this strategy account for how this category actually works? What are consumers' existing expectations in this space, and how does your strategy shift those?"

**How to fix it:**
Ground in 4Cs: How does the consumer use this category? What do competitors promise? What's the category convention your strategy breaks or honors?

---

## Using This List While Developing Your Brief

When Claude pushes back on your thinking, reference this list. It will likely be one of these patterns. Use the "How to fix it" guidance to strengthen your thinking before moving forward.

The goal isn't perfection—it's to ensure your brief is built on clear thinking that will actually guide and inspire creative work.
