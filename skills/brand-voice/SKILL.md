# /strategy:brand-voice

Discovers and documents a brand's actual voice through analysis of owned content. This is not a brief about what the brand *should* sound like — it's a forensic analysis of what it *does* sound like, what's working, what's inconsistent, and what the verbal DNA is that should be preserved and amplified.

## Philosophy

1. **Discover, don't prescribe.** The goal is to surface what already exists before recommending anything new. A brand that's been communicating for years has a voice — even if no one has written it down. Find it.
2. **Specificity over adjectives.** "Bold and authentic" describes every brand in every category. Voice DNA lives in specific word choices, sentence structures, rhythm patterns, what the brand refuses to say, and what it always comes back to.
3. **Consistency is evidence.** When a brand makes the same move in three different pieces of copy, that's not a coincidence — it's a pattern. Patterns are the voice.
4. **Inconsistency is data.** When the brand sounds completely different on LinkedIn vs. the homepage vs. the product UI, that inconsistency reveals something: either the voice has never been codified, or it's being applied selectively, or different teams are pulling in different directions.

## What It Accepts

- Owned content: website copy, campaign copy, social posts, email copy, product UI copy, press releases, long-form content
- Brand documents: existing brand guides, tone of voice documents, style guides
- Competitor copy (for contrast — how do we sound vs. the category?)
- Direct input: "Here are some examples of our voice at its best / worst"

## Process

### Phase 1: Content Collection

**If content is provided:** Read all of it before doing anything else.

**If no content is provided, ask:**

> "To discover the brand's voice, I'll need to read owned content. Can you share:
> - Website copy (homepage, about, product/service pages)
> - 3–5 campaign headlines or taglines
> - Recent social posts (10–15 ideally)
> - Any email copy or long-form content
>
> Or point me to the brand's URL and I'll pull from there."

**PAUSE for content input before proceeding.**

### Phase 2: First Read — Pattern Capture

Read all content without judgment first. As you read, capture:

**Verbal patterns:**
- Sentence structures that repeat (short declaratives? Long subordinate clauses? Questions?)
- Opening moves (how does the brand begin? Action verbs? "We"? The customer? A problem?)
- Closing moves (how does the brand end? CTA-driven? Declarative? Open?)
- Vocabulary clusters (what words appear repeatedly? What words never appear?)
- What the brand refuses to say (what competitors say that this brand avoids — or should be avoiding)

**Tonal patterns:**
- What emotional register appears most consistently?
- What relationship does the brand imply with the audience (peer? Expert? Guide? Friend?)
- Where does the brand seem most confident? Most awkward?

**Structural patterns:**
- How does the brand handle complexity? (Simplify? Acknowledge? Ignore?)
- What's the ratio of brand-first to customer-first claims?
- How much does the brand lean on proof vs. assertion?

**Inconsistencies:**
- Moments where the voice clearly breaks — content that sounds like a different brand
- Channels where the voice is noticeably weaker or stronger
- Claims that contradict each other across different surfaces

### Phase 3: Voice DNA Extraction

From the patterns, extract the Voice DNA. This is the working analysis — not yet the deliverable.

```markdown
## Voice DNA Analysis: [Brand]

### Core Voice Signature
[One paragraph describing how this brand characteristically sounds — written as an observation, not a recommendation. Specific enough that you could apply it as a filter: "Does this copy sound like [Brand]?"]

### Verbal Signatures
[3–5 specific patterns that appear consistently. Each described with examples from the content.]

1. **[Pattern name]:** [Description] 
   *Example from content:* "[Direct quote]"
   *Why this matters:* [What this pattern signals about the brand's relationship to its audience]

2. **[Pattern name]:** [Description]
   *Example from content:* "[Direct quote]"
   ...

### The Vocabulary
**Words the brand reaches for:**
[10–15 words or short phrases that appear with above-average frequency and feel characteristic]

**Words the brand avoids:**
[Words common in the category that this brand conspicuously doesn't use — if any pattern is detectable]

**Words that appear but shouldn't:**
[Industry jargon, clichés, or borrowed language that dilutes the voice]

### Tone Territory
[What emotional register the brand most consistently occupies. Specific — not "professional and friendly" but something like "the knowledgeable colleague who doesn't make you feel dumb for asking."]

**Where the tone is strongest:** [Specific content type or channel]
**Where the tone breaks down:** [Specific content type or channel, with example]

### The Brand's Relationship with Its Audience
[How does the brand position itself relative to the reader? Expert/novice? Peer? Guide? What does the copy assume about the audience's knowledge, needs, and motivations?]

### Voice Consistency Score
| Channel | Consistency | Notes |
|---------|------------|-------|
| Website | /10 | |
| Social | /10 | |
| Email | /10 | |
| Product/UI | /10 | |
| Campaign | /10 | |

*Scoring: 10 = unmistakably this brand throughout; 1 = could be any company*
```

### Phase 4: Voice Document

The deliverable. Written to be usable by writers, designers, and strategists who weren't in the room.

```markdown
# Brand Voice: [Brand]

**Version:** [Date]
**Based on:** [Content analyzed — scope and volume]

---

## Who We Sound Like

[One paragraph portrait of the brand's voice — written as if you're describing a person. Not adjectives ("bold, clear, honest") but character: how they'd show up in a conversation, what they care about, what they'd never say, what you'd walk away thinking about them. This is the compass for everything below.]

---

## The Voice in Practice

### What We Always Do

**[Move 1 — give it a name]**
[Description of the move in plain language]
✓ *Like this:* "[Example from brand's own content]"
✗ *Not like this:* "[Counter-example — either a weak version from the brand's content, or a generic version of the claim]"

**[Move 2]**
[Same structure]

**[Move 3]**
[Same structure]

*(3–5 moves. More than 5 means the voice hasn't been distilled enough.)*

### What We Never Do

**[Anti-pattern 1 — give it a name]**
[Why this is off-brand — specific to this brand, not generic writing advice]
✗ *Not this:* "[Example of the off-brand move]"

**[Anti-pattern 2]**
[Same structure]

*(2–4 anti-patterns.)*

---

## Our Words

### We reach for:
[15–20 words or short phrases. Organized by type if useful — verbs, descriptors, opening moves.]

### We avoid:
[10–15 words or phrases. Include the reason for each — "everyone uses this," "sounds arrogant," "misrepresents our tone," etc.]

---

## Tone by Context

Voice stays consistent; tone adapts to context.

| Context | Tone | Example |
|---------|------|---------|
| Product description | [Descriptor] | "[Example]" |
| Error states / bad news | [Descriptor] | "[Example]" |
| Success / celebration | [Descriptor] | "[Example]" |
| Thought leadership | [Descriptor] | "[Example]" |
| Social (short-form) | [Descriptor] | "[Example]" |

---

## Voice Test

Before publishing, ask:
1. Could this have been written by a competitor? (If yes: not specific enough.)
2. Does this sound like a person or a press release? (If press release: rewrite.)
3. Does the opening earn the next sentence? (If not: cut the opening.)
4. Is the most important thing first? (It should be.)

---

## What's Inconsistent Now

[Honest assessment of where the current brand content doesn't live up to this voice document. Not to embarrass — to give the team a clear starting point for improvement.]

| Area | Current State | What Good Looks Like |
|------|--------------|---------------------|
| [Channel or content type] | [Issue] | [Specific fix] |
| [Channel or content type] | [Issue] | [Specific fix] |
```

### Phase 5: Quality Check

| Check | Pass/Fail |
|-------|-----------|
| "Who We Sound Like" paragraph describes a person, not adjectives | |
| Every "What We Always Do" move has a real example from the brand's content | |
| Every "What We Never Do" anti-pattern is specific to this brand, not generic writing advice | |
| Word lists distinguish between "reach for" and "avoid" with rationale | |
| Tone by context table has real examples, not placeholders | |
| Voice Test questions are answerable from the document | |
| Inconsistency table is honest, not diplomatic | |

### Phase 6: Handoff and Observation

- **Save to project folder:** `outputs/brand-voice.md`
- **If Wave 2 context:** This is the primary output of the brand-voice workstream. Feed into Wave 3 synthesis for the brand-voice/owned-audit workstream.
- **If standalone:** This document feeds into any creative brief or `/strategy:brief-co-authoring` session as the voice north star.

Log per `rules/observation.md`. Track: content volume analyzed, how many channels were audited, which channel had the highest and lowest consistency score, and whether the voice emerged clearly from the content or required significant interpretation to find.

## Integration

### Brand Voice → Brief Co-Authoring
The voice document is the tone reference inside a creative brief. Every brief produced under this engagement should cite the voice document's "Who We Sound Like" and "What We Never Do" sections.

### Brand Voice → Narrative
The voice document is the editorial filter on all `/strategy:narrative` output. After producing a narrative, check it against the word lists and anti-patterns.

### Brand Voice → Wave 3
In the wave flow, the brand voice document is produced as the Wave 2 owned-audit workstream output. It feeds into Wave 3 synthesis for that workstream.

### Research → Brand Voice (competitor contrast)
Run `/strategy:research` on competitor brand voices (scrape competitor copy and analyze) to understand what the category sounds like — then identify what this brand can sound like instead.
