# The Sacred Six: Universal Brief Framework

Every brief follows this structure. Brand brief, product brief, creative brief. The depth changes. The sections do not.

Derived from the user Cole's Sacred Six, Mark Pollard's Four Points, the Xbox GamePass Creative Brief (05.02.25), and the user Alexander's sharpness standard.

---

## The Sections

### 1. THE ASSIGNMENT

**What it answers:** What are we doing? One sentence.

**The bar:** A smart outsider reads this and knows the shape of the project in 10 seconds. No jargon. No scope creep. Not a description of the deliverables. A description of the job.

**Xbox exemplar:** "A 360 brand platform that makes Gamepass a no-brainer around the world." (Score: 9.5 — "360 brand platform" is slightly activity-descriptor, but "no-brainer" redeems it.)

**Rubric:**

| Dimension | 10 | 7 | 4 | 1 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Clarity | One sentence. Anyone could repeat it. | One sentence but slightly ambiguous. | Two sentences, or one sentence with a clause that hedges. | A paragraph. Or a list of deliverables disguised as an assignment. |
| Specificity | Names the specific outcome. You know what "done" looks like. | Outcome is implied but not stated. | Describes the activity, not the outcome. ("Redesign the..." vs "Make X a no-brainer.") | Generic. Could describe any project. |

**Hard failure:** If the assignment takes more than one sentence, it's not an assignment. It's a scope document.

**When it fails — diagnose:**
- Clarity < 7: You have two ideas. Pick one. The assignment is the outcome, not the process.
- Specificity < 7: Replace the verb. "Redesign" → "Make X the obvious choice for Y." Name the outcome the client would celebrate.

### 2. THE CONTEXT

**What it answers:** Why does this matter right now? What changed?

**The bar:** This section does the heavy lifting so that every section after it stays clean. Market-level and category-level evidence lives here. The context is the setup; the problem is the punchline. Max 300 words (one page).

**The Zoom Test:** Every claim in the brief exists at one of three zoom levels:
1. **Industry/market** ("The gaming landscape has shifted to subscription models") → Context
2. **Category behavior** ("Non-competitive gamers cycle between 2-3 games monthly") → Context
3. **Individual felt experience** ("She wants to play more but feels guilty every time she picks up the controller") → Problem

Zoom levels 1 and 2 go in Context. Zoom level 3 goes in Problem. If a sentence in Context has a person as the subject, move it to Problem. If a sentence in Problem has a market or category as the subject, move it to Context.

**Xbox exemplar:** Three short paragraphs. Post-platform era shift. Gamepass as Xbox's response. Current positioning failing because of choice overload. (Score: 9 — strong narrative arc, but a few human-level details leak into the problem section.)

**Rubric:**

| Dimension | 10 | 7 | 4 | 1 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Carries the weight | All market-level and category-level evidence lives here. Problem section contains only individual felt experience. | Most data is here, but 1-2 stats leak into the problem. | Data is scattered across sections. Context doesn't do its job. | No context section. Data is everywhere or nowhere. |
| Narrative arc | Reads as a story: here's what happened, here's what it means, here's why it's urgent. | Has the right facts but reads as a list. | Facts without a "so what." | Background dump. No direction. |
| Brevity | 2-4 short paragraphs, max 300 words. Each paragraph is one idea. | Slightly long. Could lose a paragraph. | Over 400 words. Over-researched. | Either too long (research dump) or too short (missing key context). |

**Hard failure:** Over 300 words = cut it. Context is a setup, not a dossier.

**Brand architecture note:** For briefs where the subject sits within a larger brand system (sub-brand, product line, portfolio), Context must include one sentence positioning the subject relative to the parent brand. Xbox exemplar: "If XBOX's brand is about celebrating the spiritual beauty of being able to play anywhere... GamePass is about taking an active role in creating that reality."

**When it fails — diagnose:**
- Carries the weight < 7: Grep the Problem and Insight sections for numbers, competitor names, and market facts. Move them here.
- Narrative arc < 7: Restructure as three paragraphs: (1) What happened. (2) What it means. (3) Why it's urgent now.
- Brevity < 7: Cut the weakest paragraph. If all feel essential, each one is doing too much. Split into one idea per paragraph, then cut the least urgent.

### 3. THE AUDIENCE

**What it answers:** Who are we talking to? What do they do, believe, and reject?

**The bar:** Behavior, not demographics. "Males 18-34" is a media buy. "25% of men under 30 already bet online, and the first bet almost always starts in a group chat" is an audience. Name what they do. Name what they believe. Name what they'd reject. You should be able to picture this person in a room.

**Xbox exemplar:** "Global, non-competitive gamers, without an XBOX who don't see what distinct value Gamepass brings to their current gaming lifestyle." Then four behavioral segments. (Score: 7.3 — the umbrella definition leans demographic/platform-ownership; segments are behavioral but category-level. Even the exemplar's audience section is its weakest link. A 10-level audience would name specific behaviors you could observe: "They play three games this month. They wanted to play eight.")

**Rubric:**

| Dimension | 10 | 7 | 4 | 1 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Behavioral | Describes what they do, not who they are demographically. You could observe these people and confirm it. | Mostly behavioral but leans on a demographic frame. | Demographics with behavioral afterthoughts. | Pure demographics. Age, gender, income bracket. |
| Specificity | You can picture this person. Named behaviors, named beliefs, named rejections. | Clear but slightly generic. "They value quality" instead of "they'd rather wait 20 minutes for the right restaurant than settle." | Category-level description. "They want better experiences." | Could describe anyone. |
| Tension | Names what the audience wants AND what stops them. The gap between desire and reality. | Has the desire but the barrier is vague. | Describes the audience without naming a tension. | Flattering portrait with no edge. |
| Moment | Names when this person is most open to the message. A specific situation, transition, or trigger. | Implies timing but doesn't name the moment. | No temporal dimension. Static portrait. | No sense of when or where this person would encounter the brand. |

**Hard failures:**
- Pure demographic without behavioral frame = fail.
- No tension named in either Audience or Problem = fail. A brief without a barrier is a brief without a reason to exist.

**When it fails — diagnose:**
- Behavioral < 7: Delete every demographic descriptor. Rewrite starting with a verb: "They watch...", "They spend...", "They'd rather..."
- Specificity < 7: Use Pollard's comedian test. Could a comedian build a bit about this person? If the description is too flat for comedy, it's too flat for strategy.
- Tension < 7: Name the gap. "They want X. They can't because Y." If you can't write that sentence, you don't know the audience well enough. Go back to research.
- Moment < 7: Name the trigger. When are they most receptive? A life transition, a seasonal moment, a specific daily context.

### 4. THE PROBLEM

**What it answers:** What human problem are we solving?

**The bar:** One sentence, bolded. Then 2-3 sentences of felt, human-level support (internal monologue, lived moments, specific costs the person bears — NOT market data). The problem is about a person, not a market. A reader who knows nothing about the category should still nod.

**Xbox exemplar:** "They want to game more — but they are forced to choose between gaming and living." Support: two paragraphs describing the felt experience ("Hours of uninterrupted time. Expensive equipment. Needing to stay home." and "Should I play now? Ugh I only have 10 minutes."). (Score: 9.5 — the support is two paragraphs, not 2-3 sentences. But it's all zoom level 3: individual felt experience. The rubric allows this when the support stays human-level.)

**Tests:**

**Cole's test (GET/WHO/TO/BY):** Fill in the WHO slot with the problem statement. If it reads flat or like a market condition, it's not a problem. It's an observation.

**Pollard's "five whys":** Ask "why?" five times. If the problem survives all five, it's deep enough. If it collapses at "why #2," it's surface-level.

**Pollard's comedian test:** Could a comedian build a bit about this problem? If the audience would laugh in recognition, the problem is human. If it reads like a business case, it's not.

**The Zoom Test (mandatory):** If the problem sentence has a market, category, industry, or company as its subject, it belongs in Context. The Problem section's subject must be a person.

**Rubric:**

| Dimension | 10 | 7 | 4 | 1 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Human center | About a person. You feel what they feel. The problem names a cost they bear. | About a person but described from the outside. Observed, not felt. | About a market condition that affects people. | About the business, the category, or the product. |
| Compression | One sentence. Bolded. Could be read aloud in under 5 seconds. Support stays under 3 sentences of human-level detail. | Two sentences. Still clear. Support is a short paragraph. | A paragraph. Multiple ideas competing. | Multiple paragraphs. A mini-essay. |
| Felt tension | Could someone outside the category read this sentence and identify what's wrong, without any context? The problem registers on its own. | Logical but not visceral. You understand it, you don't feel it. | Requires the context section to make sense. | Abstract. Could be rewritten as "users want X" without losing meaning. |
| Specificity | Names the specific cost. "His conviction has no home" not "users lack a unified experience." | Close to specific but uses one abstraction. | Category-level language. "Fragmented experiences." | Jargon. "Suboptimal cross-vertical engagement." |

**Hard failures:**
- Subject of the problem sentence is a market, category, or company = fail. Move it to Context.
- More than two sentences in the bolded problem statement = fail.
- No barrier or cost named in either Audience or Problem = fail.

**When it fails — diagnose:**
- Human center < 7: The problem is about the business, not a person. Rewrite starting with "They..." or "He..." or "She..." Delete any sentence that names a market, category, or metric.
- Compression < 7: Pick the single strongest tension. Delete the rest. If you can't pick one, the problem isn't clear yet. Go back to Context.
- Felt tension < 7: Name the specific cost the person pays. What do they lose, miss, or give up? Fill in Cole's WHO slot. If it reads flat, the problem is abstract.
- Specificity < 7: Ban "experience," "engagement," "solution," "optimize." Replace with the exact verb the person uses. What do they actually DO?

**Cascade rule:** A weak Problem often means Context didn't do its job (the setup didn't earn the punchline). Check Context first before rewriting Problem.

### 5. THE INSIGHT

**What it answers:** What unspoken truth unlocks the opportunity?

**The bar:** A reframe. Takes the problem and flips it. The reader should think "I knew that but I never said it that way." The insight is NOT the solution. It's the lever that makes the solution obvious. One sentence. If the insight needs explaining, it's not an insight.

**Xbox exemplar:** "Gaming shouldn't force you out of your world — it should help you have more fun in it." (Score: 9.25 — clear reframe, strong recognition, points to proposition. Not quite jaw-dropping, but solid.)

**Tests:**

**Pollard's comedian test:** The best insights work like comedy premises. They name something everyone recognizes but nobody articulated. If you can imagine a comedian building a bit around the insight, it's working.

**The Reversal Test (mandatory):** Write the problem and insight side by side. Check:
- Does the insight use the same subject as the problem? (If yes, suspect restatement.)
- Does the insight use the same verb structure? (If yes, suspect restatement.)
- Does the insight change who is acting on whom, or what the relationship is?

Xbox example:
- Problem: "They're forced to choose between gaming and living." (Subject: they. Verb: forced to choose. Relationship: gaming vs. living.)
- Insight: "Gaming shouldn't force you out of your world — it should help you have more fun in it." (Subject: gaming. Verb: should help. Relationship: gaming enhances living.)
- Subject shifted from person to product. Verb shifted from "choose" to "help." Relationship reversed from opposition to partnership. That's a real reframe.

**The "because" test (hard failure):** If the insight and the problem can be connected with the word "because" without losing meaning, the insight is a causal explanation, not a reframe. "They're forced to choose because gaming pulls them away from life" — that's not an insight. That's why the problem exists. An insight changes the frame, not the cause.

**Rubric:**

| Dimension | 10 | 7 | 4 | 1 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Reframe | Changes how you see the problem. Before and after. The problem looked one way; now it looks like an opportunity. | Adds nuance to the problem but doesn't flip it. | Restates the problem in different words. | A fact, not an insight. |
| Recognition | Names a behavior or belief the audience already exhibits, framed in a way the audience wouldn't articulate themselves. | Interesting but doesn't trigger the "I knew that" response. | Surprising but detached from the audience's lived experience. | Academic. True but inert. |
| Compression | One sentence. No supporting text needed. | One sentence plus one sentence of support. | A paragraph. | Multiple paragraphs. Not an insight — an analysis. |
| Generative | The proposition becomes obvious once you read the insight. It points to one clear direction. | Points in a direction but doesn't narrow to one proposition. | Interesting but doesn't tell you what to do. | Dead end. True but not actionable. |

**Hard failures:**
- Passes the "because" test (can be connected to problem with "because") = restatement, not insight.
- Uses the same subject-verb structure as the problem = restatement, not insight.
- Is a data point ("61% of Americans see prediction markets as gambling") = evidence, not insight.

**When it fails — diagnose:**
- Reframe < 7: Write the problem's subject-verb-relationship. Now write the opposite or an unexpected alternative for each. The insight lives in the reversal.
- Recognition < 7: Go back to the audience section. What do they already do that proves this insight is true? If you can't find the behavior, the insight is aspirational, not real.
- Generative < 7: If the insight doesn't point to the proposition, either the insight is too broad or the proposition doesn't follow from it. Fix the connection, don't force it.

**Cascade rule:** A weak Insight almost always means the Problem isn't sharp enough to reframe. Fix Problem first before rewriting Insight.

### 6. THE PROPOSITION (Rallying Cry)

**What it answers:** What are we doing about it? 2-5 words.

**The bar:** A sticky note. Someone who heard it once can repeat it at a bar. It names a choice a competitor wouldn't make. The sacrifice is visible. A designer can gut-check a layout against it. A developer can resolve an edge case against it.

**Xbox exemplar:** "More Play." Two words. Then one supporting sentence: "GamePass helps you play in more moments. So you can experience more." (Score: 8.75 — compression 10, memorability 10, actionability 8. Sacrifice is the weakest dimension at 7: PlayStation Plus could plausibly claim "More Play." The sacrifice — choosing breadth of moments over depth of sessions — is implied rather than named. For brand platforms, the sacrifice may be visible in the positioning rather than stated in the proposition.)

**Tests:**

**Competitor Claim Test (mandatory):** Fill in this table before scoring sacrifice:

```
SACRIFICE VERIFICATION
───────────────────────────────────────
Proposition: [yours]
Competitor A: [name] → Would they claim this? Y/N → What do they claim instead?
Competitor B: [name] → Would they claim this? Y/N → What do they claim instead?
Competitor C: [name] → Would they claim this? Y/N → What do they claim instead?

If any Y → score sacrifice at 4 or below and rewrite.
If you can't fill "what they claim instead" → research the category first.
```

**Cole's "choosing against" test:** The proposition is a strategic choice. What are you choosing NOT to do? If you can't name the alternative strategy you rejected, you haven't made a choice. You've written a tagline.

**Directionality check:** Cover the proposition. Re-read sections 1-5. Does the brief still feel incomplete? If the problem and insight point naturally to the proposition, it passes. If the problem feels reverse-engineered to justify the proposition, rewrite the problem from scratch without looking at section 6.

**Rubric:**

| Dimension | 10 | 7 | 4 | 1 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Compression | 2-5 words. Feels inevitable after the insight. | 5-8 words. Close but could be tighter. | A full sentence. | A paragraph. Not a proposition — a strategy statement. |
| Sacrifice | Names what you're giving up. Competitor Claim Test returns N for all competitors. | Implies a sacrifice but doesn't name it. Competitors wouldn't claim it but sacrifice is implicit. | No sacrifice. Any brand in the category could claim it. | Actively avoids making a choice. |
| Memorability | Sticks after one read. You'd put it on a wall. | Clear but not sticky. You'd need to look it up. | Requires context to understand. | Forgettable. Corporate. |
| Actionability | A designer can check a layout against it. A developer can resolve an edge case. It's a decision-making tool, not a slogan. | Directional but not specific enough to resolve decisions. | Inspiring but not functional. | Decorative. |

**Hard failures:**
- More than 5 words = not a rallying cry.
- Competitor Claim Test returns Y for any competitor = rewrite.
- Can't name what you're choosing against = not a strategy.

**When it fails — diagnose:**
- Compression < 7: Delete words until it breaks. The last version before it breaks is your rallying cry.
- Sacrifice < 7: Run the Competitor Claim Test. If competitors could claim it, you haven't made a choice. Name the opposite of your proposition. That's what you're sacrificing.
- Memorability < 7: Say it out loud. If you stumble, it's too complex. If it has more than one clause, cut.
- Actionability < 7: Test it. "Does this [design/feature/copy] create more [proposition]?" If the question doesn't resolve the decision, the proposition is too abstract.

## Supporting Sections

### WHAT WE'RE NOT DOING

**What it answers:** What's explicitly out of scope? What would a reasonable person assume is in scope — but isn't?

**The bar:** 3-7 bullets. Each one saves real time and prevents real arguments. The best exclusions are the ones that make someone uncomfortable — the thing the client expects, the thing the team assumes, the thing that "obviously" should be included. If the exclusions are safe, they're not doing their job.

| Dimension | 10 | 7 | 4 | 1 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Courage | At least one exclusion makes someone uncomfortable. Names the sacred cow. | Reasonable exclusions but nothing controversial. | Safe picks only. "We're not doing things we were never going to do." | Missing or generic. |
| Specificity | Each exclusion is one sentence, named and specific. | Mostly specific, one or two are vague. | Category-level. "Out of scope: things not in scope." | Bullet-pointed jargon. |

### SUCCESS

**What it answers:** How do we know it worked?

**The bar:** 3-5 measurable outcomes. Numbers, not vibes. Each metric has a timeframe. "Strong adoption" fails. "50,000 active users within 90 days" works.

| Dimension | 10 | 7 | 4 | 1 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Measurability | Every metric has a number and a date. You could build a dashboard. | Most metrics are measurable. One is vibes. | Mix of measurable and aspirational. | "Users love it." |
| Relevance | Metrics trace directly to the proposition. Success measures whether the rallying cry worked, not whether the project shipped. | Metrics are reasonable but generic (downloads, revenue). | Vanity metrics. | No metrics. |

### DELIVERABLES & MANDATORIES (optional, for briefs received by creative/design teams)

**What it answers:** What is the team actually making? What format constraints exist?

**The bar:** A bulleted list of what the team produces. Format, quantity, platform, any creative constraints. Not strategy — logistics.

**Xbox exemplar:** "The GamePass: _____ line" plus Round 1 expectations (idea line, statement, manifesto, adlob, gameplay extension) plus titles to concept against.

Include this section when the brief will be received by a creative, design, or build team. Omit for internal strategy documents.

## The Quality Gate Process

Sections with more dimensions are intentionally harder to ace. Problem and Proposition carry the most weight. This is by design.

Sacred Six sections: no section below 6/10. Supporting sections contribute to the total but don't have individual minimums.

### Step 1: Crystallization Test (mandatory, run FIRST)

Write the GET/WHO/TO/BY line:
- **GET** [target audience, behavioral]
- **WHO** [the problem, from their POV]
- **TO** [desired action or shift — what changes for the audience]
- **BY** [what the brand/product does — the proposition in action]

If you can't write this line clearly, the brief is structurally broken. Fix the broken section before scoring anything.

**Worked example (Xbox):**
- **GET** non-competitive gamers who don't see GamePass's value
- **WHO** want to game more but are forced to choose between gaming and living
- **TO** see GamePass as the thing that lets them play in more moments
- **BY** removing the barriers between gaming and the rest of their life ("More Play")

**Broken crystallization examples:**
- WHO slot is a market condition ("face a fragmented landscape") → Problem isn't human-centered. Fix Problem.
- TO slot is vague ("feel better about the brand") → Proposition isn't doing work. Fix Proposition.
- BY slot restates the WHO ("by solving their fragmentation problem") → No insight. The insight should create the bridge between WHO and BY.
- GET slot is demographic ("males 18-34") → Audience isn't behavioral. Fix Audience.

**Note on TO vs. BY:** TO is the audience's shift (what changes for them). BY is the brand's move (what we do to cause that shift). If these blur together, the proposition isn't distinct from the problem.

### Step 2: Self-Score (mandatory)

Score each of the Sacred Six on its rubric dimensions. Calculate a per-section score (average of dimensions, each 1-10).

```
SACRED SIX QUALITY GATE
────────────────────────────────────────────────
1. Assignment      ___/10  (clarity + specificity)
2. Context         ___/10  (weight + arc + brevity)
3. Audience        ___/10  (behavioral + specificity + tension + moment)
4. Problem         ___/10  (human + compression + tension + specificity)
5. Insight         ___/10  (reframe + recognition + compression + generative)
6. Proposition     ___/10  (compression + sacrifice + memorability + actionability)
────────────────────────────────────────────────
Total:             ___/60

Supporting:
7. Not Doing       ___/10  (courage + specificity)
8. Success         ___/10  (measurability + relevance)
────────────────────────────────────────────────
Grand Total:       ___/80

PASS: 56/80 (70%) overall, no Sacred Six section below 6/10.
HARD STOP: Problem below 7/10 or Proposition below 7/10 = rewrite those sections.
```

### Step 2.5: Whitespace Check (mandatory, after self-score)

Run the whitespace audit from `references/whitespace-check.md`. This verifies the positioning is genuinely unique, not shared territory with a paint job.

For each positioning claim (proposition, emotional core, audience, insight, key exclusions), test against 3-5 competitors:
- Could they SAY this? (messaging)
- Could they DO this? (product exists or ships in 6 months)
- Would they WANT to? (fits their audience/strategy)
- If all three Y: NOT WHITESPACE.

Then map the intersection: which combination of claims is unmatched?

Rate defensibility per claim: **Structural** (can't copy without becoming a different company), **Temporal** (head start), or **Cosmetic** (ships next quarter).

```
WHITESPACE VERDICT
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Uniqueness:      ___/10  (7+ required)
Defensibility:   ___/10  (7+ required)
Specificity:     ___/10  (7+ required)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Below 7 on Uniqueness = rewrite the proposition.
Below 7 on Defensibility = flag the timeline risk.
```

Dispatch 3 parallel competitor agents for coverage: test 2 competitors each.

### Step 3: Sharpness Pass (mandatory, stacks on self-score)

Run the sharpness rubric from `~/.claude/feedback/strategy/sharpness-rubric.md`.
Brevity, plain language, human center, single idea, compression. Must hit 35/50.

### Step 4: Voice + Polish (mandatory for client-facing output)

Run voice-dna.md (Layer 1) and copy-polish.md (Layer 2) line by line.
Both must hit 35/50 per existing rules.

### Step 5: Present to the user

Include the scorecard, the Crystallization Test line, and the Competitor Claim Test table. Flag any dimension below 8. The brief has passed four checks before the user sees it.

## Failure Recovery

### Rewrite Limits

Two rewrite attempts per failing section. If the section still fails after two rewrites, flag it for User:
"Section [X] is scoring [Y/10]. The weak dimensions are [Z]. I've revised twice. Here are both attempts. Want me to continue or adjust the direction?"

### Cascade Rules

Fix upstream before rewriting downstream. Failures cascade:
- Weak **Context** → almost always produces a weak **Problem** (the setup didn't earn the punchline)
- Weak **Problem** → almost always produces a weak **Insight** (nothing to reframe)
- Weak **Insight** → almost always produces a weak **Proposition** (no lever to build on)

When a section fails, check the section above it first. Don't polish a broken foundation.

## Courtroom Escalation Triggers

The courtroom (/strategy:courtroom) is a heavy tool — 10 agents across two rounds. Do NOT use it on every brief. Auto-escalate if ANY of these are true:

```
COURTROOM ESCALATION TRIGGERS
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1. SCORE-BASED: Insight or Proposition scored below 7 on ANY single
   dimension (not just the average — one weak angle = one blind spot)

2. SACRIFICE FAILURE: The Competitor Claim Test returns Y for any
   competitor. The proposition needs adversarial pressure.

3. INSIGHT SUSPICION: The Reversal Test or "because" test fails.
   The insight might be a restatement. Contrarian + Reductionist confirm.

4. STAKES-BASED: The brief will be seen by anyone outside the immediate
   team (client, C-suite, board, press). Binary check. No judgment call.

5. NOVEL CATEGORY: Fewer than 3 briefs written in this category.
   Unfamiliarity increases blind spots.
```

When the courtroom runs on a brief, it debates three specific questions:
1. Is the problem real? (Empiricist + Audience agents)
2. Is the insight a genuine reframe or a restatement? (Contrarian + Reductionist agents)
3. Could a competitor claim the proposition? (Competitor + Cynic agents)
