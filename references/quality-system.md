# Strategy Quality System

Two layers: a quick-check list that catches mechanical failures, and a scoring rubric that evaluates strategic depth. Both run before presenting any output.

---

## Layer 1: Quick Checks

Run on every section of the strategy doc and every slide of the slide-ready copy. Any failure = fix before presenting.

### Tension Check
- [ ] Does the strategy create a felt problem before offering a solution?
- [ ] Is there a named tension between two forces (brand vs culture, false choice vs reframe, core vs growth)?
- [ ] Would a reader feel the problem viscerally, or just understand it intellectually?
- [ ] If you removed the solution, would the problem alone be compelling?

### Specificity Check
- [ ] Is every insight specific to THIS brand in THIS cultural moment?
- [ ] Could you swap in a different brand name and have the strategy still work? If yes, it's too generic. Rewrite.
- [ ] Are cultural references named and dated (a specific tweet, trend, behavior, data point), not vague ("consumers today...")?
- [ ] Are audience descriptions based on behavior and belief, not just demographics?

### Argument Check
- [ ] Does each section build on the previous one, or could they be rearranged?
- [ ] Is there a single clear line from problem → insight → strategy → expression?
- [ ] Does the strategy feel earned by the argument, or bolted on?
- [ ] Is the coined concept a genuine compression of the insight, or a label stuck on top?

### Consultant-Speak Check
- [ ] Any sentence that sounds smart but says nothing? Kill it.
- [ ] Any framework that exists to look rigorous rather than clarify thinking? Simplify or cut.
- [ ] Any use of: "ecosystem," "360," "end-to-end," "holistic," "synergy," "paradigm," "stakeholder alignment"? Replace with plain language.
- [ ] Any sentence a CMO has heard 100 times before? ("Consumers are looking for authenticity," "The brand needs to meet people where they are," "We need to create meaningful connections") Rewrite with the specific thing.

### Cultural Grounding Check
- [ ] Is the cultural truth actually true right now, or is it a recycled observation?
- [ ] Would someone living inside the culture recognize this as their experience?
- [ ] Is the cultural reference doing strategic work (driving the insight), or is it decoration (making the deck feel current)?

### Copy Quality (run stop-slop)
- [ ] All checks from stop-slop apply. Run the full stop-slop Quick Checks list on every line.
- [ ] Strategy writing has additional traps: false authority ("brands must..."), prediction without evidence ("the future of X is Y"), and category-level claims presented as insights ("Gen Z values authenticity").

## Layer 2: Scoring Rubric

Score the complete output on 5 dimensions (1-10 each). **Below 35/50: revise before presenting.**

| Dimension | Question | What 10 looks like |
|-----------|----------|-------------------|
| **Tension** | Does the strategy create a problem worth solving before solving it? | The reader feels stuck in the problem. The tension is specific, cultural, and urgent. Removing the solution section would leave a compelling provocation. |
| **Specificity** | Is every claim grounded in this brand, this audience, this moment? | Named cultural signals, real quotes, specific competitive gaps. No sentence could apply to a different brand. Audience descriptions feel like portraits of real people. |
| **Narrative Arc** | Does the argument build, or does it list? | Each section earns the next. The insight feels inevitable in retrospect but surprising when you reach it. The strategy is the only logical conclusion of the argument. |
| **Cultural Grounding** | Is the cultural context real, current, and load-bearing? | Cultural references do strategic work, not decoration. The strategy would collapse without them. Someone inside the culture would nod, not roll their eyes. |
| **Persuasion** | Would this change someone's mind? | The reader arrives at the strategy feeling like they discovered it themselves. The argument closes escape routes. The coined concept sticks after you close the doc. |

### Scoring Notes

- **8-10**: Exceptional. This section/output is doing real strategic work.
- **6-7**: Solid but could be sharper. One or two elements are carrying weight; others are filling space.
- **4-5**: Generic. The structure is right but the content could apply to multiple brands/situations.
- **1-3**: Consultant filler. Sounds professional but says nothing a smart person couldn't guess.

### How to Apply

1. Score each dimension
2. Flag any dimension below 7 with the specific section and what's weak
3. If total is below 35: identify the root cause (usually weak tension or generic insight) and restructure before line-editing
4. If total is 35-40: line-edit the weak sections
5. If total is above 40: present to the user

## Strategy-Specific Stop-Slop Additions

Beyond the standard stop-slop banned phrases, strategy writing has its own failure modes:

### Banned Strategy Phrases

- "Consumers are looking for..." (name the specific behavior)
- "The brand needs to meet people where they are" (name the specific place)
- "Create meaningful connections" (connections to what, through what)
- "Brands that X will win" (which brands, what evidence)
- "In an increasingly [adjective] world" (name the specific change)
- "The future of X is Y" (whose future, based on what signal)
- "Authenticity" used without defining what's authentic about THIS brand
- "Purpose-driven" without naming the purpose
- "Cultural relevance" without naming the specific culture
- "Resonate with audiences" (which audiences, through what mechanism)
- "Tap into" anything (tap into culture, tap into a trend, tap into a need)
- "White space" without drawing the actual map
- "Ownable" without proving no one else can claim it

### Banned Strategy Structures

- **The vague manifesto**: Inspirational language that reads like a brand video script but contains no strategic direction. If you can't extract a decision from it, it's decoration.
- **The insight that isn't**: Observations are not insights. "People use their phones a lot" is an observation. "Phone dependency has made spontaneity feel dangerous" is an insight. Test: does it reveal a tension that suggests an action?
- **Strategy by adjective**: Defining a brand by stacking adjectives (bold, authentic, innovative, human-centered) instead of making a choice. Strategy is what you choose NOT to do.
- **The false inevitability**: Presenting a strategy as the only logical outcome when the argument hasn't earned it. If the reader can imagine a different conclusion from the same evidence, the argument needs tightening.
